Conservation of a tracer (fluid dynamics "review")

Consider a tracer with a concentration C(x,y,z,t)C(x,y,z,t)C(x,y,z,t) (with units like g/m3\mathrm{g/m^3}g/m3) that is advected by a flow with velocities u=(u,v,w)\mathbf{u} = (u,v,w)u=(u,v,w), where uuu is the velocity in the xxx direction, vvv in the yyy direction, and www in the zzz direction.

Now consider a volume V=δxδyδzV = \delta x \delta y \delta zV=δxδyδz that is a fixed cube in space. The rate of change of tracer in the volume is given by the sum of transport of tracers across the six faces into the volume:

∫V∂C∂t dV=−∫SFC⋅n dS\int_V \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} \, dV = -\int_S \mathbf{F_C} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, dS ∫V​∂t∂C​dV=−∫S​FC​⋅ndS

where FC\mathbf{F_C}FC​ is the flux of CCC, SSS is the surface of the volume and n\mathbf{n}n is the outward normal vector to the surface. Note that the transport has units like g/s, and the flux FCF_CFC​ has units like g/m^2/s.

There are two types of fluxes that can change CCC, the advective flux and the diffusive flux. The advective flux is that due to the flow of the water carrying the tracer and is given by

FC=Cu\mathbf{F_C} = C \mathbf{u} FC​=Cu

Supposing for a second that diffusive fluxes are negligible, we can write the equation as

∫V∂C∂t dV=−∫SCu⋅n dS\int_V \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} \, dV = -\int_S C \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, dS ∫V​∂t∂C​dV=−∫S​Cu⋅ndS

Diffusive fluxes

Diffusive fluxes are those due to the random motion of the tracer molecules, and are given by Fick's law:

FC=−K∇C\mathbf{F_C} = -K \nabla C FC​=−K∇C

where KKK is the diffusivity of the tracer (with units like m^2/s). The diffusivity is a measure of how fast the tracer spreads out in space. For example, for temperature, K≈10−7m2/sK \approx 10^{-7} \mathrm{m^2/s}K≈10−7m2/s, and for salt, K≈10−9m2/sK \approx 10^{-9} \mathrm{m^2/s}K≈10−9m2/s. Substituting this into the conservation equation gives:

∂C∂t=−∇⋅(uC)+K∇2C\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = - \nabla \cdot \left(\mathbf{u}C\right) + K \nabla^2 C ∂t∂C​=−∇⋅(uC)+K∇2C

This is the advection-diffusion equation for a tracer CCC.

  • All mixing takes place at the molecular level, but the diffusivity co-efficient is very small.
    • tends to act on small-scales
    • turbulent stirring can enhance the fluxes, and is often parameterized as an "eddy diffusivity" or "turbulent diffusivity" KTK_TKT​,
    • KTK_TKT​ is much larger than molecular, but acts on larger scales.

Conservation of water mass

The conservation of water mass can be written:

∂ρ∂t+∇⋅(ρu)=diffusion\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{u}) = \mathrm{diffusion} ∂t∂ρ​+∇⋅(ρu)=diffusion

Ignoring diffusion, we can re-write as:

1ρ(∂ρ∂t+u⋅∇ρ)=−∇⋅u\frac{1}{\rho} \left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} +\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\rho\right) = - \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} ρ1​(∂t∂ρ​+u⋅∇ρ)=−∇⋅u

  • density of seawater only changes by 3% or so in the ocean, so the left hand side is small, or

∂ρ∂t+u⋅∇ρ≈0\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} +\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\rho \approx 0 ∂t∂ρ​+u⋅∇ρ≈0

  • so the right hand side is also small, or ∇⋅u≈0\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} \approx 0∇⋅u≈0.
  • this is called the incompressibility approximation and is a very good approximation for the ocean.
    • it is much less good for the atmosphere, but sometimes gets used there as well if flows are not too vertical.
  • The integral form of this is:

  • ∫Su⋅n dS=0\int_S \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, dS = 0 ∫S​u⋅ndS=0

  • or the net flow of water into a volume is zero.

Eulerian vs Lagrangian reference frames:

Physical laws are defined following a parcel of water (Lagrangian) For the above, the physical law is that the change of tracer following the fluid is due to diffusion:

DCDt=K∇2C\frac{D C}{D t} = K\nabla^2 C DtDC​=K∇2C

From the above, we see that if the flow is incompressible, then

DρDt=∂ρ∂t+u⋅∇ρ=0\frac{D \rho}{D t} = \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\rho = 0 DtDρ​=∂t∂ρ​+u⋅∇ρ=0

DCDt\frac{D C}{D t}DtDC​ is the material derivative of CCC, and is the change of CCC following a parcel of water. It is also called the Lagrangian derivative.

The laws above were derived fixed in space, and so we derived an equation for the Eulerian derivative of CCC:

∂C∂t+u⋅∇C=K∇2C\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla C = K\nabla^2 C ∂t∂C​+u⋅∇C=K∇2C

As an example, consider a stream flowing at speed UUU in the xxx direction, and suppose the tracer is high upstream and low downstream. Ignoring diffusion, if we follow the stream at a speed UUU, then the tracer will not change. If we are sitting stationary on the bank, then ∂C/∂t=−U∂C/∂x\partial C/\partial t = -U\partial C/\partial x∂C/∂t=−U∂C/∂x, which will be positive because dC/dx<0dC/dx < 0dC/dx<0 (the tracer is high upstream and low downstream).

Conservation of momentum

The conservation of momentum is given by Newton's second law:

  • what are the forces?
    • pressure gradient forces
    • viscous stress forces
    • Coriolis force
    • gravity force
    • The first two are interfacial forces that act on the edges of a given fluid volume.
    • The last two are body forces that act on the whole volume.

Pressure gradient force:

The pressure gradient force is a huge part of what gives fluids a very different class of behaviour than solids. If water piles up somewhere, it creates pressure gradients inside the fluid that can drive accelerations. Note this is not just the piled up water pouring downhill - the pressure gradient is felt throughout the water column, so even deep water will move.

  • a demo using a small tank is very nice here, with a dye streak to show that all the water moves, not just the surface when there is a wave in the tank.

Estimating pressure: hydrostatic approximation

In general in fluid dynamics, pressure can be "dynamic" and due to the motion of the fluid. However, if vertical accelerations are small compared to the acceleration due to gravity, then we can assume that the pressure is hydrostatic:

1ρ∂P∂z=−g−DwDt≈−g\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial P}{\partial z} = - g - \frac{Dw}{Dt} \approx -g ρ1​∂z∂P​=−g−DtDw​≈−g

if ∣Dw/Dt∣≪g|Dw/Dt| \ll g∣Dw/Dt∣≪g.

  • except for surface waves, and vigorous convection, this approximation is very good in the ocean, and therefore we can calculate the pressure at a depth zzz if we know the density and the seasurface height η\etaη

P(z)=P(η)+∫zηρg dz′P(z) = P(\eta) + \int_z^{\eta} \rho g \, dz' P(z)=P(η)+∫zη​ρgdz′

where P(η)P(\eta)P(η) is the sealevel pressure (usually near 10 dbar or 10^5 Pa) at the ocean surface.

Note that if the density is constant, then this simplifies to

P(z)=P(η)+ρg(η−z)P(z) = P(\eta) + \rho g (\eta - z) P(z)=P(η)+ρg(η−z)

and note that z<0z<0z<0 so the pressure increases as we go deeper.

The way to think about this is that the pressure at depth zzz is the force per unit area needed to support the weight of the water above it, which is the weight of the water column from zzz to the surface η\etaη.

Note that in the x- and y-momentum equations we have terms like

1ρ∂P∂x\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} ρ1​∂x∂P​

If the flow is hydrostatic, then

DuDt=−1ρ∂P(η)∂x−1ρ∂∂x∫zηρg dz′\frac{Du}{Dt} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial P(\eta)}{\partial x} - \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \int_z^{\eta} \rho g \, dz' DtDu​=−ρ1​∂x∂P(η)​−ρ1​∂x∂​∫zη​ρgdz′

which we can use the Leibniz rule to write as

DuDt=−1ρ∂P(η)∂x−∂η∂xg+1ρ∫zη(x)∂ρ∂xg dz\frac{Du}{Dt} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial P(\eta)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial x} g + \frac{1}{\rho}\int_z^\eta(x) \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x} g\,\mathrm{d}z DtDu​=−ρ1​∂x∂P(η)​−∂x∂η​g+ρ1​∫zη​(x)∂x∂ρ​gdz

For most practical purposes the first term is small because atmospheric sea level pressure doesn't vary much, and does so over large areas. In the third term, the contribution of the density gradient from z=0toηz=0 to \etaz=0toη is small, so we simplify to:

DuDt=−∂η∂xg−1ρ0∫z0∂ρ∂xg dz\frac{Du}{Dt} = - \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial x} g - \frac{1}{\rho_0}\int_z^0 \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x} g\,\mathrm{d}z DtDu​=−∂x∂η​g−ρ0​1​∫z0​∂x∂ρ​gdz

The first term on the right hand side is the acceleration due to sea surface height gradients, and is called the barotropic pressure gradient force. The second term is the acceleration due to density gradients, and is called the baroclinic pressure gradient force.

As an example of the baroclinic pressure gradient force, in the above sketch, the weight of the water at A is less than the weight of the water column at B, so the pressure at A is less than the pressure at B, and therefore there is a net pressure gradient from from B towards A, and the water in the bottom layer will accelerate to the left.

  • soon after there will be change in the surface pressure gradient as water piles up at A and is removed from B.

Viscous stress (shear stress)

Viscous stresses are related to pressure, in that they arise from molecules hitting each other and transferring momentum. Viscous stress is the portion of those stresses that are due to the relative motion of the fluid.

  • Consider a fluid with two layers, one moving at speed UUU and the other at speed 000.
    • There are no "shear stresses" inside the layers because the molecules are, on average, moving with each other.
    • The interface between the two layers has a shear stress as some of the "fast" molecules hit the "slow" ones and transfer momentum from the upper layer to the lower.
  • We use the notation τxz\tau_{xz}τxz​ for this shear stress, where xxx is the direction of the flow and zzz is the vertical direction, and the sign convention that τxz>0\tau_{xz}>0τxz​>0 to mean that positive x-momentum is being transferred downwards (from the upper layer to the lower in this example).

Of course the same idea applies in all the directions (including the x-direction), so the x-momentum equation has three shear stress terms τxx\tau_{xx}τxx​, τxy\tau_{xy}τxy​, and τxz\tau_{xz}τxz​, which we will sometimes write as τx=(τxx,τxy,τxz)\mathbf{\tau_x} = (\tau_{xx}, \tau_{xy}, \tau_{xz})τx​=(τxx​,τxy​,τxz​).

  • These terms get used in the x-momentum equation by noting that on a small cube, the shear stress can be exerted on the top face by the water above, and is lost on the bottom face to the water below

  • So the x-momentum equation becomes:

and as the cube is made infintesimally small, this becomes:

ρDuDt=∂τxz∂z+...\rho \frac{D u}{Dt} = \frac{\partial \tau_{xz}}{\partial z} + ... ρDtDu​=∂z∂τxz​​+...

  • including the oter directions yields:

ρDuDt=∂τxx∂x+∂τxy∂y+∂τxz∂z+...\rho \frac{D u}{Dt} = \frac{\partial \tau_{xx}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \tau_{xy}}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial \tau_{xz}}{\partial z} + ... ρDtDu​=∂x∂τxx​​+∂y∂τxy​​+∂z∂τxz​​+...

ρDuDt=∇⋅τx+...\rho \frac{D \mathbf{u}}{Dt} = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{\tau_x} + ... ρDtDu​=∇⋅τx​+...

Newtonian fluid shear stresses

It is often useful to write the momentum equation using the shear stresses, and we will do so in later classes. However, it is also useful to assume the fluid is a Newtonian fluid, which means that the shear stresses are proportional to the velocity gradients:

τxz=μ∂u∂z\tau_{xz} = \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial z} τxz​=μ∂z∂u​

where μ\muμ is the dynamic viscosity of the fluid (with units like kg/m/s). The dynamic viscosity is a measure of how "sticky" the fluid is, and for water it is about 10−3kg m−1 s−110^{-3} \mathrm{kg\,m^{-1}\,s^{-1}}10−3kgm−1s−1 at 20 deg C. The shear stresses in the other directions are given by similar equations:

τxy=μ∂u∂y\tau_{xy} = \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} τxy​=μ∂y∂u​

τxx=μ∂u∂x\tau_{xx} = \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} τxx​=μ∂x∂u​

or in vector form:

τx=μ∇u\mathbf{\tau_x} = \mu \nabla \mathbf{u} τx​=μ∇u

Note that the momentum equation is almost always written in terms of the kinematic viscosity ν=μ/ρ\nu = \mu/\rhoν=μ/ρ, which has units like m^2/s.

This means that the momentum equations become (including the pressure gradient force):

DuDt=−1ρ∂P∂x+ν∇2u+...\frac{D u}{Dt} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + ... DtDu​=−ρ1​∂x∂P​+ν∇2u+...

DwDt=−1ρ∂P∂z−g+ν∇2v+...\frac{D w}{Dt} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - g + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{v} + ... DtDw​=−ρ1​∂z∂P​−g+ν∇2v+...

or in vector form:

DuDt=−1ρ∇P+ν∇2u−gk+...\frac{D \mathbf{u}}{Dt} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla P + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} - g\mathbf{k} + ... DtDu​=−ρ1​∇P+ν∇2u−gk+...
